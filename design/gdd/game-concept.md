# Gaia Chronicles — Game Concept Document

**Status**: Approved  
**Engine**: Unreal Engine 5.7  
**Genre**: Narrative Puzzle Adventure  
**Platform**: PC (primary), Console (future)  
**Perspective**: Third-person exploration

---

## Elevator Pitch

A young Maine Coon named Gaia sets out as an oral historian for her matriarchal cat-people
tribe, tasked with verifying the scattered ruins of a post-apocalyptic world. Each ruin hides
a memory fragment from a different era — and together they reveal that the founding myth of
her civilization has been quietly, deliberately rewritten.

---

## World Overview

### The Collapse

Decades ago, resource wars between male-dominated power blocs collapsed civilization. The
conflict mirrored the worst impulses of the old world: hoarding, scorched-earth tactics,
and territorial violence that poisoned land, water, and trust. The event is called
**The Burning**.

### The Exodus

Small groups survived by fleeing the war zones. The escape was chaotic, dangerous, and
bloody — a road movie through a dying world (tonal reference: *Mad Max: Fury Road*). The
survivors who made it were overwhelmingly female, children, and a few males who had
refused to fight.

### The New Dawn

The survivors founded the **Okaeri Collective** — a loose federation of cat-people tribes
organized on matrilineal principles, inspired by real-world Mosuo social structure
(泸沽湖摩梭族). Children belong to their mother's household. Property and leadership pass
through the female line. Men participate fully in society but do not hold collective
governance roles. The system was not imposed — it was chosen, deliberately, by the first
generation, as a conscious rejection of what came before.

Two generations later, the Collective is thriving, stable, and beginning to mythologize
its own origins.

## Protagonist — Gaia

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Species** | Maine Coon (long-haired, silver-gray tabby, amber-gold eyes) |
| **Age** | Early twenties |
| **Role** | Known troublemaker. No official role — that's the point. |
| **Personality** | Impulsive, irreverent, allergic to being told what not to do. Sharp when she needs to be. Gets into trouble not out of malice but out of relentless curiosity and a short fuse for received wisdom. |
| **Visual reference** | Dignified but slightly disheveled — ornate inherited jewelry worn carelessly over practical travel clothes. The jewelry matters more than she lets on. |
| **Starting state** | Nowhere near ready to be a protagonist. Pushed by accident, not destiny. |

### Character Arc — "The Princess Diaries Model"

Gaia's arc is driven by *events*, not by internal heroism. She does not volunteer for any of this.

**Act 1 — Pushed:** Gaia sneaks into a ruin that is officially off-limits (boredom + a dare + the
fact that she was told not to). She accidentally triggers a Memory Fragment that she was never
meant to see. The fragment contradicts something central to the founding myth. She can't
un-know it, but she also has no framework for what to do with it. She tries to go back to
her life. She can't.

**Act 2 — Pulled:** The first fragment opens questions she can't stop pulling at. She starts
returning to ruins not to find the truth but to *understand the one thing she already saw*.
Each discovery stops feeling like an accident. She's still messy, impulsive, wrong a lot —
but she starts caring, and that caring is new and slightly embarrassing to her.

**Act 3 — Chosen:** By the end, Gaia is not a reluctant hero. She has chosen — actively,
with full awareness of the cost — to see this through. Not because she was destined to.
Because she decided she was the one who had to. This is the moment the game is building toward.

Gaia is not a hero archetype. She's not trying to save the world. She stumbles into a truth
that someone has been carefully burying, and she has to decide what to do with it — but
the *stumbling* comes first. The deciding is earned.

## Core Mechanic — Memory Fragments

**How it works:**

The player explores present-day ruins as Gaia. When she touches specific objects (artifacts,
carvings, architecture), she experiences a **Memory Fragment** — a playable scene from a
different era, rendered in a distinct visual style.

Memory Fragments are not cutscenes. The player inhabits the perspective of a character
from that time and completes a short puzzle or observational task. The fragment then ends,
and the player returns to Gaia in the present — who now understands the ruin differently.

**Four time layers:**

| Layer | Era | Visual Style | What It Shows |
|-------|-----|--------------|---------------|
| **A — The Old World** | Pre-Collapse | Warm sepia, decaying opulence | How the collapse started from the inside |
| **B — The Road** | During the Exodus | Harsh contrast, ash-gray palette | The actual events of the escape |
| **C — The Founding** | Early Okaeri | Handmade warmth, rough textures | How the matriarchal system was negotiated — and what was compromised |
| **D — Gaia's Present** | Now | Full UE5 color, rich world | Main exploration layer; accumulates meaning as A/B/C are revealed |

Each time the player returns from a fragment, Gaia has new context to reinterpret the
environment. Some ruins change visually (objects rearrange, hidden passages appear) after
key fragments are viewed.

## Real-World Archaeological Anchors

Each major area is designed around a real historical site. The connection is thematic and
architectural — not a literal recreation.

| Game Area | Real Inspiration | Core Theme |
|-----------|-----------------|------------|
| **Sourceholm** (Hub city) | Çatalhöyük, Turkey (~9000 BCE) | Was the "egalitarian utopia" always as equal as the myth says? |
| **The Mirror Coast** | Lugu Lake / Mosuo homeland, Yunnan | Walk-marriage, inherited households, what "family" means without a patriarch |
| **The Labyrinth** | Knossos Palace, Crete (Minoan) | A powerful woman at the center of a myth that was rewritten by its enemies |
| **Dry Vein** | Garamantian underground water network, Sahara | A civilization that controlled water controlled everything — and then didn't |
| **Ironroot Shrine** | Göbekli Tepe, Turkey (~12000 BCE) | Humans built temples before cities. What does that say about what we actually need? |

## Central Mystery

The founding myth credits a council of seven women with establishing the Okaeri Collective
after the Exodus. This is true — but incomplete.

There was an eighth person: **a male survivor** who played a decisive role in negotiating
the peace between the earliest rival groups. He was present at the founding. He chose,
voluntarily, to step back from the historical record — because he understood that the new
society needed an unambiguous symbol, and his presence in the story would be used to
undermine it.

He asked to be forgotten. They agreed.

The question the game asks is not: *was this wrong?* The game asks: **now that Gaia knows,
what does she do with it?** And: **does a story need to be literally true to be
functionally true?**

There is no single correct ending. The game has three:
1. **Restore** — Gaia documents the full truth. The Collective fractures briefly, then stabilizes with a more complex origin story.
2. **Protect** — Gaia buries her findings. The myth holds. She carries it alone.
3. **Release** — Gaia doesn't publish her findings, but leaves them where someone else will eventually find them.

## Engine Recommendation Notes

- **UE5.7 with Lumen**: The lighting transitions between time layers (sepia → ash → handmade warmth → full color) are a core artistic feature. Lumen's dynamic GI is essential for this.
- **Nanite**: Ruins with high geometric detail are a visual pillar. Nanite handles the density of carved stonework without LOD pop.
- **Blueprint for fragment triggers**: Memory Fragment entry/exit logic is well-suited to Blueprint event graphs with a C++ backing class for state management.
- **UMG for dialogue/journal**: The journal UI (where Gaia records her findings) and the dialogue system both use UMG with CommonUI for gamepad support.
